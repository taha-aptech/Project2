Rabies Virus

Rabies virus (RABV) is transmitted through direct contact (such as through broken skin or mucous membranes in your eyes, nose, or mouth) with saliva or brain/nervous system tissue from an infected animal. Rabies is fatal but preventable. It can spread to people and pets if they are bitten or scratched by a rabid animal.
Rabies virus gets into your body when the saliva (spit) of an infected animal gets into an open wound (usually from a bite). It moves very slowly along nerves into your central nervous system (your brain and spinal cord). When it reaches your brain, the damage causes neurological symptoms. From there, rabies leads to coma and death.
Rabies is most common in rural parts of Asia and Africa, though itâ€™s found on all continents except Antarctica. In the U.S., rabies is commonly found in wild animals. But dogs carry rabies in many other countries. Children are more likely to get rabies than adults. Rabies moves from an infected wound to your brain over time. There are several phases that most people go through: incubation, prodromal phase, acute neurologic phase and coma.

Rabies Virus Information

Aspect Information
What is Rabies Virus? Rabies is a viral disease that causes encephalitis in humans and other mammals. It was historically referred to as hydrophobia because its victims would panic when offered liquids to drink.
First Outbreak The first written record of rabies is from around 1930 BC in the Mesopotamian Codex of Eshnunna. The earliest evidence of rabies in humans and dogs dates back to 2300 BC in the Mosaic Esmuna Code of Babylon.
Symptoms Initial symptoms: Flu-like symptoms like fever, headache, weakness, and discomfort. You might also feel prickling, itching, or discomfort at the bite site.
Brain inflammation: As the disease progresses, the brain and spinal cord become inflamed. This can lead to confusion, anxiety, delirium, hallucinations, and agitation.
Behavioral changes: You might experience hyperactivity, excitable behavior, or strange behavior.
Fear: You might develop a fear of water (hydrophobia) or a fear of fresh air (aerophobia).
Muscle spasms: You might experience painful spasms in your throat and larynx when you try to drink.
Paralysis: You might be unable to move.
Difficulty swallowing or breathing: You might have difficulty swallowing or breathing.
Diagnosis Several tests are necessary to diagnose rabies antemortem (before death) in humans; no single test is sufficient. Tests are performed on samples of saliva, serum, spinal fluid, and skin biopsies of hair follicles at the nape of the neck.
Treatment Rabies immune globulin: An injection that provides immediate protection while the vaccine starts working.
Rabies vaccine: A series of shots given over 14 days.
Wound washing: Thoroughly cleansing the wound may reduce the likelihood of developing rabies
Prevention Rabies can be prevented by reducing exposure to rabid animals, vaccinating pets, and seeking prompt medical care if exposed
Vaccine Two types of vaccines to protect against rabies in humans exist - nerve tissue and cell culture vaccines. WHO recommends replacement of nerve tissue vaccines with the more efficacious, safer vaccines developed through cell culture as soon as possible.
What is rabies?
Rabies is a deadly viral disease that affects the nervous system of mammals, including humans. It spreads through the saliva of infected animals, usually via bites.
How does rabies spread?
Rabies spreads through bites, scratches, or when saliva from an infected animal enters open wounds, eyes, nose, or mouth. It cannot spread through air or casual contact.
Which animals can carry rabies?
Dogs, cats, bats, raccoons, foxes, skunks, and monkeys are common carriers. Stray or wild animals have a higher risk of carrying the virus.
What are the symptoms of rabies in humans?
Early symptoms include fever, headache, weakness, and tingling near the bite. Later, symptoms worsen to anxiety, hallucinations, difficulty swallowing, paralysis, and death.
How long does rabies take to show symptoms?
Symptoms usually appear within 1 to 3 months after exposure, but in some cases, they can appear within a few days or take more than a year.
What should I do if an animal bites or scratches me?
Wash the wound immediately with soap and running water for 10â€“15 minutes. Then, seek medical attention as soon as possible for post-exposure rabies treatment.
Is there a cure for rabies?
No, once symptoms appear, rabies is almost always fatal. However, early treatment with post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) can prevent the disease from developing.

Safety Tips

Report Animal Bites or Scratches

If you are bitten or scratched by an animal, wash the wound immediately with soap and running water

Vaccinate Your Pets

Ensure your pets, including dogs, cats, and even ferrets, receive regular rabies vaccinations.

Avoid Contact with Stray and Wild Animals

Never approach, touch, or feed stray dogs, cats, or wild animals such as bats, raccoons, foxes, or monkeys. .
