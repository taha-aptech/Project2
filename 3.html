Cholera

Cholera is an acute diarrhoeal infection that can kill within hours, if left untreated. Cholera remains a global public health threat, causing between 1.3 million to 4.0 million cases, and 21,000 and 143,000 deaths worldwide every year. It is caused by ingestion of food or water contaminated with the bacterium Vibrio cholerae.
Symptoms appear 12 hours to five days after ingesting contaminated food or water, and it can kill within hours if untreated. The signs and symptoms include rice water stools, fishy odour stools, vomiting, rapid heart rate, loss of skin elasticity, dry mouth, low blood pressure, dehydration, muscle cramps, restlessness or irritations especially in children, sleepiness or tiredness. However, some infected people may not show any symptoms.
Prevention and control can be achieved by consuming clean water and food, and by providing proper hygiene conditions and access to basic services. Adopting basic hygienic behaviours such as regular handwashing with soap after defecation and before handling food or eating, and safe preparation and conservation of food are essential. Vaccination with Oral Cholera Vaccines (OCV) has proved effective in cholera control. The primary treatment for cholera is the use of oral rehydration salts (ORS) and the use of antibiotics.

CHOLERA INFORMATION

Aspect Information
What is Cholera? A bacterial disease that causes severe diarrhea and dehydration.
First Outbreak Cholera was first recorded in 1817 in India.
Symptoms Diarrhea, vomiting, dehydration, muscle cramps, low blood pressure, dry mouth, and sunken eyes.
Diagnosis Cholera is diagnosed through a stool sample, which is tested for Vibrio cholerae bacteria.
Treatment The primary treatment is oral rehydration solution (ORS), with antibiotics used in severe cases.
Prevention Ensuring access to clean water and sanitation, proper hygiene practices, and vaccination.
Vaccine Oral Cholera Vaccines (OCV) are available and provide short-term protection against cholera.
Who Discovered Cholera?
The First Discovery: Filippo Pacini, 1854
In 1854, the year of the notorious cholera outbreak in London investigated by John Snow, the disease also struck mainland Europe. Italy was not spared, and cholera outbreaks occurred in several cities and towns of the peninsula.

What are the Symptoms?
Symptoms include diarrhea, vomiting, leg cramps, fever, and drowsiness.
When is cholera most likely to occur?
Cholera outbreaks are more likely to occur in areas with poor water, sanitation, and hygiene. They can also occur after natural disasters like hurricanes or earthquakes.
Who is at risk?
People who don't have access to clean water or sanitation are at the highest risk of getting cholera.

Safety Tips

Drink Safe Water

Use boiled, filtered, or bottled water for drinking and cooking. Avoid untreated water.

Practice Good Hygiene

Wash hands with soap and clean water before eating, cooking, and after using the toilet.

Eat Safe Food

Eat freshly cooked food. Avoid raw or undercooked seafood, street food, and unpeeled fruits.
