1885 - Rabies Vaccine Developed

In 1885, Louis Pasteur and Émile Roux developed the first rabies vaccine, which proved to be successful in preventing the disease after exposure to the virus.

  • The first effective rabies vaccine was developed by Pasteur and Roux.
  • This vaccine marked a major breakthrough in the prevention of rabies, saving countless lives.
  • It became the first successful treatment for rabies after exposure.

1905 - Rabies Serum for Post-Exposure Treatment

In 1905, a rabies serum was developed to further enhance the treatment of individuals who had been exposed to rabies. This serum was used in conjunction with the rabies vaccine.

  • The development of rabies serum allowed for better post-exposure treatment.
  • This serum was used to complement the vaccine and offered more effective prevention of the disease.

1950 - Global Rabies Control Efforts Begin

In the 1950s, global efforts to control rabies started to take off, with a strong focus on vaccinating domestic animals, especially dogs, which were the primary source of rabies transmission to humans.

  • Mass vaccination campaigns for dogs were initiated to prevent rabies transmission to humans.
  • Public health efforts were ramped up to raise awareness about the importance of vaccination and control.

1970 - Rabies Surveillance and Control Programs Expanded

In the 1970s, rabies surveillance and control programs were significantly expanded globally. More countries began establishing national programs to reduce the prevalence of the disease.

  • WHO began formal rabies surveillance programs to track cases globally.
  • Mass vaccination efforts were introduced in various countries to prevent the spread of rabies.

1990 - Rabies Vaccine Improvements

During the 1990s, rabies vaccines were significantly improved in terms of safety and efficacy. Shorter treatment regimens for post-exposure prophylaxis were developed, making rabies treatment more accessible.

  • New, more effective rabies vaccines were developed with fewer side effects and faster treatment schedules.
  • Increased access to vaccines improved rabies prevention efforts globally.

2024 - Rabies Control and Elimination Efforts

As of 2024, global efforts to eliminate rabies continue, with a focus on improving access to vaccines, surveillance, and increasing public awareness to prevent fatalities.

  • Continued efforts to eliminate rabies worldwide with ongoing vaccination campaigns.
  • Advances in oral vaccines for wildlife are being used to reduce rabies transmission.
  • Increased focus on prevention in countries where rabies remains a major public health concern.
