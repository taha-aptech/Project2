1981 - First Reported Cases

In June 1981, the CDC reported a rare lung infection in five young men in Los Angeles, marking the first recognized cases of what later became known as AIDS.

  • First cases identified in the U.S.
  • Initially associated with homosexual men.
  • Termed "Gay-Related Immune Deficiency" (GRID) before being recognized as AIDS.

1990s - Advancements in Treatment

By the 1990s, the global spread of HIV was a major public health crisis. The introduction of antiretroviral therapy (ART) dramatically changed treatment outcomes.

  • 1995 - First protease inhibitors approved.
  • 1996 - ART therapy led to a major decline in HIV-related deaths.
  • 1999 - WHO estimated that HIV/AIDS was the fourth biggest cause of death worldwide.

2000s - Global Response Strengthens

The early 2000s saw the expansion of global HIV/AIDS programs and funding for treatment in developing nations.

  • 2003 - U.S. launched PEPFAR to combat HIV/AIDS worldwide.
  • 2005 - WHO and UNAIDS accelerated global access to ART.
  • 2008 - HIV confirmed to have originated from a type of chimpanzee.

2010s - Towards Ending HIV/AIDS

Efforts to control the epidemic intensified with improved ART access and research on a potential cure.

  • 2012 - FDA approved Truvada for PrEP (HIV prevention).
  • 2017 - UNAIDS aimed for 90-90-90 global targets.
  • 2019 - London patient became the second person to be cured of HIV.

2020s - Ongoing Research & Hope

Despite advancements, challenges remain. However, newer treatments and vaccine trials continue to offer hope.

  • 2021 - Third patient cured of HIV via stem cell transplant.
  • 2023 - mRNA-based HIV vaccine trials launched.
  • 2024 - Global efforts continue to eradicate HIV/AIDS.
